# Studio sulla Dissoluzione Timbrica

**Studio sulla Dissoluzione Timbrica** is an electroacoustic composition for an eight-channel / octophonic system.

The project was developed by **Giovanni Maria Vona** as an academic work for the course of **Esecuzione e Interpretazione della Musica Elettroacustica II** within the First Level Academic Diploma in **Tecnico del Suono** at the Conservatorio Statale di Musica “O. Respighi” in Latina.

The piece explores the progressive dissolution of timbral identity through the interaction between concrete sound materials, electronic synthesis, spectral transformation and multichannel spatialization.

## Overview

The central idea of the composition is the gradual transformation of recognizable sound sources into increasingly unstable, abstract and spatially diffused sonic materials.

The project investigates the perceptual threshold between:

- recognizable source identity
- electroacoustic transformation
- spectral residue
- abstract timbral texture
- spatial dissolution

The process of dissolution is not conceived only as a subtractive reduction of the sound material. It is instead developed through a combination of filtering, modulation, spectral recoloring, degradation, granular transformation and spatial stratification.

The work is closely related to the concept of **source bonding**, focusing on how the listener’s perception of a sound source can be progressively destabilized through electroacoustic transformation.

## Artistic Concept

The composition is built around a continuous relationship between:

- **foreground material**
- **background texture**
- **granular off-screen layer**
- **spatial diffusion**

The foreground material is mainly derived from concrete sound sources and is subjected to progressive timbral erosion. A secondary background layer, derived from the same concrete materials, is introduced as a semi-continuous granular texture.

This background layer does not function as a simple accompaniment. It acts as a structural element that contributes to the continuity of the piece and to the construction of a deeper perceptual space.

The idea of timbral dissolution therefore involves several dimensions at the same time:

- spectral transformation
- dynamic instability
- loss of source recognizability
- spatial diffusion
- ambiguity between figure and background

The piece aims to explore the gradual passage from recognizable sonic gesture to unstable perceptual object.

## Sound Materials

The composition is based on the interaction between concrete and synthetic sound materials.

### Concrete Materials

The concrete materials were recorded in studio and selected for their spectral richness, internal variability and suitability for electroacoustic transformation.

The main sources include:

- cymbals
- classical guitar
- percussive materials
- resonant and unstable instrumental gestures

The recordings focus on non-conventional gestures such as:

- cymbal brushing
- cymbal resonances
- friction sounds
- non-percussive attacks
- unstable or prolonged sounds

These materials were chosen because they are rich in internal micro-variations and can be transformed without immediately losing their perceptual complexity.

Particular importance is given to cymbal brushing material, which is also used as the basis for the granular background texture introduced in the second section of the piece.

### Synthetic Materials

The synthetic materials derive mainly from an analog synthesizer and are used as extensions of the concrete material rather than as independent sound objects.

The main synthetic sources include:

- white noise
- modulated noise
- analog delay feedback
- sine wave material

The synthetic layer contributes to the spectral transformation of the piece and reinforces the progressive loss of clear source identity.

In the first section, sine wave material is related to the classical guitar material. Later, it is transformed through distortion, spectral modification and frequency shifting processes.

## Techniques

The project uses several electroacoustic transformation techniques, including:

- spectral filtering
- resonant band-pass filtering
- parametric equalization
- amplitude modulation
- ring modulation
- low-frequency modulation
- pitch shifting
- tape-stop-like slowing processes
- BBD delay and dynamic feedback
- flanger and chorus processing
- spring reverb
- sidechain-controlled gates
- granular processing
- microtemporal fragmentation
- spectral degradation

These techniques are not used as isolated effects, but as tools for the progressive transformation of the sound material.

## Subtractive Synthesis and Spectral Filtering

Subtractive synthesis plays a central role in the project.

Dynamic filters and parametric equalizers are used to reshape the spectral content of both concrete and synthetic materials. Resonant band-pass filtering is particularly important, since it allows specific spectral regions to emerge from the original sound.

The filter does not simply remove frequencies. It acts as a perceptual focusing device, making hidden or secondary components of the sound temporarily more evident.

This principle is also extended to the live performance environment, where a resonant filtered layer is controlled in real time.

## Granular Texture and Background Layer

Granular processing is used to transform concrete sound material into semi-continuous textures.

The granular background layer is derived mainly from cymbal brushing material. Through fragmentation and microtemporal reorganization, the original gesture becomes less recognizable and assumes the role of an unstable, diffuse and peripheral sound field.

This layer becomes especially important from the second section onward, where it contributes to the distinction between:

- foreground
- background
- off-screen sound space

The gradual degradation of this layer contributes to the overall process of timbral and perceptual dissolution.

## Formal Structure

The piece lasts approximately **7’16”** and is organized into three main sections.

### I. Presentation and Articulation of the Material  
**0’00” – 2’04”**

The first section introduces the main sound materials and establishes the initial gestural vocabulary of the piece.

This section begins with concrete material, presented through short gestures, micro-variations and repetitions. The sound sources are still relatively recognizable.

A gradual transition toward synthetic material follows. Concrete and synthetic sounds begin to overlap, especially through the relationship between classical guitar, noise-based material and sine wave components.

The section ends with a stronger focus on synthetic material, completing the exposition of the main timbral areas of the composition.

### II. Transformation and Timbral Dissolution  
**2’04” – 5’03”**

The second section intensifies the transformation processes.

The previously introduced materials are subjected to more radical spectral, temporal and morphological modifications. The musical discourse becomes more continuous, with longer units and a stronger focus on internal transformation rather than surface-level contrast.

A granular background texture is introduced and remains active throughout the section. This layer creates a semi-continuous, wave-like and peripheral sound field.

At the same time, the foreground material is progressively eroded through resonant filtering, spectral transformation and dynamic processing.

This section represents the central area of the piece’s transformation process.

### III. Residue and Rarefaction  
**5’03” – 7’16”**

The third section presents a more advanced state of timbral dissolution.

The sound materials are now distant from their original identities and are perceived mainly as traces, residues or unstable configurations.

The granular background persists, but becomes more irregular, fragmented and unpredictable. The distinction between foreground and background becomes increasingly unstable.

Processes such as degradation, glitch, extreme filtering, reverberation and microtemporal transformation contribute to the loss of clear source identity.

This final section represents the point of greatest distance from the initially recognizable sound materials.

## Spatialization

The piece is conceived for an **eight-channel / octophonic diffusion system**.

Spatialization is not treated as a decorative element, but as a structural part of the composition. The spatial behavior of the materials follows the same conceptual process as the timbral transformation.

The spatial organization is based on:

- gradients of presence
- differentiated distribution of sound sources
- foreground/background stratification
- progressive spatial diffusion
- reduction of source localization
- perceptual depth

The spatialization avoids overly spectacular or gesture-based movements. Instead, it focuses on presence, distance, density and spatial transformation.

The main spatial tendencies are:

- cymbal materials occupy a more frontal region of the system
- classical guitar materials are progressively oriented toward a more rearward position
- synthetic materials are distributed more uniformly
- the granular background layer moves in a wave-like way between odd and even channels
- the final section tends toward a more uniform and less hierarchical spatial image

The progressive reduction of spatial distinction mirrors the dissolution of timbral identity.

## Realization Environment

The composition was produced mainly in **Reaper**, which was used for:

- recording
- editing
- organization of sound materials
- preliminary processing
- formal construction
- multichannel spatialization
- preparation of the eight-channel rendered version

The live performance and diffusion component was developed in **Max/MSP**.

## Max/MSP Performance Environment

The repository includes a Max/MSP performance environment designed for the octophonic execution and perceptual transformation of the piece.

The patch is not intended as a generative system that creates the composition in real time. Instead, it functions as a performance and diffusion environment for a pre-rendered eight-channel version of the piece.

The system is based on:

- playback of the eight-channel audio file using `mc.sfplay~`
- direct multichannel routing
- parallel signal processing
- resonant band-pass filtering
- real-time control of spectral focus
- temporal decorrelation of the processed layer
- summing of original and filtered material
- octophonic output routing

## Parallel Filtering System

The Max/MSP patch duplicates the multichannel signal into two paths:

```text
eight-channel audio file
        ↓
 ┌───────────────┬────────────────────┐
 │ direct signal │ parallel filtered layer │
 └───────────────┴────────────────────┘
        ↓
 octophonic output
```

The direct signal preserves the original eight-channel composition.

The parallel layer is processed through resonant band-pass filtering and then mixed back with the original signal.

This filtered layer is used as a live perceptual focus tool, allowing the performer to emphasize specific spectral regions of the piece during diffusion.

## Decorrelation

Before filtering, the parallel layer is slightly decorrelated through a fractional delay line implemented inside an `mc.gen~` subpatch.

This process reduces direct correlation between the original and processed signals, limiting unwanted phase interference and comb filtering when the two layers are summed.

The fractional delay is implemented through linear interpolation between adjacent samples, allowing continuous sub-sample delay values.

## Performance Control

The live performance focuses on the control of a limited number of perceptually important parameters:

- filtered signal level
- resonant filter frequency
- resonant filter Q
- background / off-screen layer level
- delay time of the decorrelated layer

The performance does not introduce new sound material. It acts on the way the already composed material is perceived during diffusion.

The resonant filter functions as a kind of spectral lens: it isolates, emphasizes and temporarily brings forward specific regions of the multichannel sound material.

Through this process, the performance extends the compositional idea of timbral dissolution into the act of listening and diffusion.

## Requirements

To open and use the performance environment:

- Max/MSP
- multichannel audio interface
- eight-channel loudspeaker system for full octophonic performance
- stereo monitoring system for the stereo reduction

The stereo MP3 reduction can be listened to without the Max/MSP patch.

## Usage

### Listening to the stereo reduction

Open the stereo MP3 file located in:

```text
audio/
```

This file is a stereo reduction of the original eight-channel composition.

### Opening the Max/MSP performance patch

1. Open the main patch located in the `max/` folder.
2. Make sure that the eight-channel audio file is available.
3. Configure the audio output routing according to the available multichannel system.
4. Load the eight-channel audio file in the playback system.
5. Use the exposed controls to shape the filtered parallel layer during performance.
